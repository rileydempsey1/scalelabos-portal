The hot seat is voluntary but high-value. One member per call brings their biggest current constraint and the group spends 20 minutes on it. Book your first hot seat in week 3 or 4. Don't wait for it to feel comfortable.

Find one other member in a similar situation and set up a 30-minute call between group sessions. The best relationships in this room don't come from the weekly calls. They come from conversations that start on the weekly calls and continue offline.

This is not a social club. It is not a place to come and vent. It is not a motivational subscription. If you need someone to tell you you're doing great, this is the wrong room. If you need someone to tell you exactly where you're bleeding money and how to stop it, you're in the right place.

**One request:**

## SECTION 2: WEEKLY GROUP CALL CONTENT (8 WEEKS)

*Each call is 60 minutes. One hot seat per call. Host leads weeks 1-2, facilitator rotation begins week 3.*

### WEEK 1: What Are Your Real Numbers?

**Theme:** Financial visibility. Most operators at $1M-$5M are running on gut feel. This session establishes the baseline.

**Pre-work** (sent 48 hours before):
- Pull last 90 days of revenue by month
- Know your current cash balance
- Know your gross margin on at least 3 jobs from the last 60 days

**60-Minute Agenda:**

| Time | Segment |
|------|---------|
| 0:00-0:05 | Host opens: who is in the room, what this call is for |
| 0:05-0:15 | Member introductions: name, revenue range, number one problem right now (60 seconds each) |
| 0:15-0:25 | Host teaches: the three numbers every operator must know cold (revenue run rate, gross margin %, cash on hand) |
| 0:25-0:45 | Hot seat: one member shares their financial snapshot, group asks questions, identifies one thing to fix |
| 0:45-0:55 | Open Q&A on financials |
| 0:55-1:00 | Homework assignment and close |

**Three Host Questions:**
1. "What's your gross margin on your best job in the last 90 days, and what made it different from your worst?"
2. "If I asked you right now what your net margin was last month, what would you say? And how confident are you in that number?"
3. "When did you last raise your prices? What happened to your close rate when you did?"

**Hot Seat Rotation Rule:** Week 1 host selects a volunteer from intake survey respondents who marked cash flow or pricing as their top constraint. Starting week 2, hot seat is assigned 5 days in advance. Member receives 3 prep questions so they come prepared, not reactive.

**Post-Call Homework:**
Fill in the Overhead Calculator. Come to Week 2 knowing your true overhead monthly. If you don't have clean books, use your best estimate and note the gaps.

---

### WEEK 2: Where Are Your Deals Dying?

**Theme:** Sales conversion. Most operators at $2M-$4M are losing 40-60% of deals at the estimate stage and don't know it. I ran Roof King at a 60% deal-loss rate at the estimate stage for 18 months before I tracked it.

**Pre-work:**
- Run the Sales Conversion Tracker on your last 20 leads
- Know your close rate from lead to signed contract
- Identify the last 3 deals you lost and what the stated reason was

**60-Minute Agenda:**

| Time | Segment |
|------|---------|
| 0:00-0:05 | Open and recap of week 1 homework (who ran their overhead number?) |
| 0:05-0:15 | Host teaches: the 5-stage sales funnel and where money leaks at each stage |
| 0:15-0:35 | Hot seat: member walks through last 20 leads on Conversion Tracker, group identifies leak point |
| 0:35-0:50 | Group drill: role-play one price objection using the script library (host leads, members rotate) |
| 0:50-0:58 | Open Q&A |
| 0:58-1:00 | Homework and close |

**Three Host Questions:**
1. "At what stage do most of your leads drop off, and what's your follow-up process at that exact stage?"
2. "What's your average time from first contact to estimate, and from estimate to follow-up? Are those intentional numbers or just what happens?"
3. "Name one deal you lost in the last 60 days where you knew the real objection. What was it, and what did you say?"

**Hot Seat Rotation Rule:** Assigned member comes prepared with their last 20 leads tracked: lead source, date contacted, estimate sent (yes/no), follow-up (yes/no), outcome (won/lost/pending). This is not optional for the hot seat member.

**Post-Call Homework:**
Identify the stage where you're losing the most leads. Write your current process at that stage in one paragraph. Bring it to week 3.

### WEEK 3: What's Leaking in Production?

**Theme:** Production variance and job costing. Members routinely find $40K-$180K per year sitting in production variance. A single job audit at Roof King found $2,800 in one job, which was 9 margin points that went unnoticed.

**Pre-work:**
- Pull your last 5 completed jobs: estimated cost vs. actual cost
- Know your average production variance
- Identify your highest-variance job in the last 90 days

**60-Minute Agenda:**

| Time | Segment |
|------|---------|
| 0:00-0:05 | Open and recap week 2 homework |
| 0:05-0:15 | Host teaches: the 4 most common production leaks (material waste, crew hours, change orders, scope creep) |
| 0:15-0:35 | Hot seat: member presents job costing data, group identifies largest leak |
| 0:35-0:50 | Workshop: build a job kickoff checklist for the hot seat member's business |
| 0:50-0:58 | Q&A |
| 0:58-1:00 | Homework and close |

**Three Host Questions:**
1. "What's the gap between your highest-margin job and your lowest-margin job from the last 90 days? What caused it?"
2. "Who on your team owns production variance? Is there a specific person accountable for the job hitting estimate?"
3. "If you audited one job right now, where would you expect to find the leak?"

**Hot Seat Rotation Rule:** Hot seat member must present at least 3 jobs with estimated vs. actual line items. If they don't have this data, the hot seat becomes: building the system to capture it.

**Post-Call Homework:**
Run the Production Leak Calculator on your last 10 jobs. Bring the variance number to week 4.

| 0:58-1:00 | Homework and close |

**Three Host Questions:**
1. "What's your longest outstanding invoice right now, and have you called that customer in the last 7 days?"
2. "When does your draw schedule release cash, and is that tied to your actual cost exposure or just milestones?"
3. "If I asked you to tell me your cash position 60 days from now, could you give me a number? What would it take to know that?"

**Hot Seat Rotation Rule:** Any member with over 45 days average collection gets priority for hot seat rotation in this week.

**Post-Call Homework:**
Call your two slowest-paying customers this week using the AR collection script from Section 3. Bring the outcome to week 5.

### WEEK 5: Where Are Your Leads Coming From?

**Theme:** Lead generation, source tracking, cost per lead, and building a repeatable machine. Most operators have no idea which lead source drives their best close rate.

**Pre-work:**

3. "If you had to double your leads in 90 days with no additional ad spend, what would you do first?"

**Pre-work:**

- Know your current pricing model
- Know your average job size and average margin

**60-Minute Agenda:**

| 0:50-0:58 | Q&A |
| 0:58-1:00 | Homework and close |

2. "What's your breakeven revenue number? How close to it are you running right now?"
3. "In the last 12 months, have you raised your prices? If not, your effective margin has already shrunk because costs don't stay flat."

**Hot Seat Rotation Rule:** Hot seat member must come with their overhead number (from the calculator) and at least one recent job with full cost breakdown.

**Post-Call Homework:**

### SALES SCRIPTS

---

#### Sales 1: Cold Inbound Call
*Use when: customer calls from a yard sign, ad, referral, or Google. They want a quote.*

---

"Thanks for calling [company name], this is [your name]. What can I help you with today?"

*[Let them explain. Don't interrupt. Take notes.]*

"Got it. And when did you first notice this?"

*[They answer.]*

"Has anyone else looked at it yet?"

*[If yes: "What did they say?" If no: "Good. Let's get someone out there before it goes further."]*

"I want to get the right person out to take a look and give you an honest assessment. It won't cost you anything for us to come out, and at the end we'll tell you exactly what you're dealing with and what your options are. You'll never feel pressured. We're not going to come out and sell you something you don't need.

What does your schedule look like this week? We can usually get someone out within 24 to 48 hours."

*[They give availability.]*

"Perfect. I'm going to put you down for [day/time]. I need a good address and the best number to reach you at. We'll send a confirmation text the morning of.

One thing I always like to ask before we hang up: is there anything specific you want us to look at while we're there, or anything that would help us understand the full picture when we come out?"

*[They answer. This builds the inspection agenda and shows you're listening.]*

"Great. We'll see you [day/time]. My name is [name], and if anything comes up before then, here's my direct number: [number]. Any questions before I let you go?"

---

*Notes: The goal of this call is one thing: set the appointment. Don't estimate over the phone. Don't give price ranges. The phrase "honest assessment" and "you'll never feel pressured" are trust builders that close more appointments than urgency language. Average call length should be 4 to 7 minutes.*

---

#### Sales 2: Hot Inbound Call (Storm / Hail Event)
*Use when: a storm or hail event just happened. Customer calls in response to canvassing, ads, or a neighbor referral. Insurance job likely.*

---

"Thanks for calling [company name], this is [your name]. Are you calling about the storm that came through?"

*[They confirm.]*

"Good. You're smart to call now. Here's what I want you to know before we even talk about scheduling: the longer you wait to get a professional assessment, the harder your insurance company can make the claim process. Not because of anything we do, just because time creates documentation gaps.

What's the address, and have you already filed a claim with your insurance company?"

*[If no claim filed yet:]*

"Here's my honest recommendation: let us come out first and do a full assessment at no cost. If we find legitimate damage, we'll walk you through exactly what to document before you call your adjuster. If we don't find damage, I'll tell you that too, and you won't have a claim on your record.

Does that make sense?"

*[If claim already filed:]*

"Perfect. Have they given you a claim number yet? We're going to want that on file. And do you have a date set for the adjuster visit yet?"

*[If adjuster already visited:]*

"Okay. What did they say? And do you have a copy of their scope? We want to review it alongside our own findings. Adjusters miss things and we supplement for what they missed all the time."

*[Set appointment.]*

"I want to get someone out within the next 24 hours if possible. These windows fill up fast after storms. What's your address and what time works for you today or tomorrow?"

---

*Notes: Move fast on storm calls. Every other contractor in town is calling the same neighborhood. The differentiator is not price. It is process knowledge. Customers who understand the insurance claim process close at a significantly higher rate because you've de-risked the decision. Don't use high-pressure language. Educate first.*

---

#### Sales 3: Re-Engaging a 60-Day Cold Lead
*Use when: a prospect got an estimate 60+ days ago and never signed. No contact since.*

---

"Hi, is this [name]? This is [your name] with [company name]. We came out and looked at [brief description of their project, their property, etc.] back in [month]. I'm not calling to pressure you. I just want to check in.

A lot of people who get an estimate from us and don't move forward have a reason. Sometimes the timing is off. Sometimes they're comparing quotes. Sometimes something came up. I'm not here to overcome any of that. I'm just checking in to see where you're at.

Has anything changed with the [property/project] since we talked?"

*[They answer. Listen. Don't jump to a solution yet.]*

*[If they say they went with someone else:]*
"Got it. I appreciate you letting me know. If anything comes up with that work or you need anything in the future, I'd love to earn the shot. Mind if I ask what made you go with them?"

*[If they say they haven't done anything yet:]*
"Okay. What's been holding you up? No wrong answer."

*[They explain. Then:]*

"I hear that. Here's what I can do: I'll have someone reach back out to walk through the estimate again and answer any questions. If the situation has changed, if the damage has gotten worse or if your timeline has shifted, we can update the numbers. If everything still looks the same and you're just not ready, that's completely fine.

Would it be helpful to have that conversation?"

*[Set a follow-up call or revisit if they're open.]*

---

*Notes: Tone is the entire script here. Don't start the call by referencing the old estimate price. Don't lead with a discount. Most 60-day cold leads didn't move because of a friction point you don't know yet. This script surfaces that point. The operator who listens best here closes the re-engagement.*

---

#### Sales 4: 3-Touch Follow-Up Sequence
*Use after: estimate is sent. Customer goes quiet. Three separate touchpoints.*

---

**Touch 1: Day 1 after estimate sent (same day or next morning):**

"Hi [name], this is [your name] from [company]. I just wanted to make sure you received the estimate I sent over. Sometimes these end up in spam. Can you confirm you got it? If you have any questions on any line item, I'm happy to walk through it with you. You can reach me directly at [number]."

*[Text version of the same message if no answer.]*

---

**Touch 2: Day 3:**

"Hi [name], following up on the estimate for [brief project description]. I want to make sure you have everything you need to make a decision. Sometimes people have questions they don't want to bother asking. Nothing is too small. If you have a competing quote and want to compare them side by side, I'm happy to do that with you. Just reply here or call me at [number]."

---

**Touch 3: Day 7:**

"[Name], last follow-up from me on this. I don't want to keep filling up your phone. If the timing isn't right or you went another direction, I get it. Just let me know so I can close out the file. If you're still deciding, I want to make sure you have the information you need. My number is [number]. Either way, I appreciate the chance to come out."

---

*Notes: The three-touch sequence does two things: it keeps you top of mind, and it de-escalates the follow-up relationship. Touch 3 uses "permission to close the file" language, which psychologically often prompts a response from people who were ignoring the first two. Never follow up more than 3 times without a response unless they re-engage. After touch 3 with no response, move them to the 60-day re-engagement list (Sales Script 3).*

---

#### Sales 5: Price Objection: "That's Too High"
*Use when: customer sees the estimate and says the price is too high, without a specific competing number.*

---

"I hear that. Can I ask you a question before we talk about price?

When you say it's too high: is that relative to what you were expecting based on the size of the job, or relative to a number someone else gave you?"

*[Wait for the answer. This is important.]*

*[If it's just sticker shock, no competing quote:]*

"Fair. Let me walk you through where the number comes from. The materials on this job are [X]. Labor is [Y]. And our overhead recovery, which is what it costs us to run a business that shows up on time, has insurance, has a warranty, and doesn't disappear after the job. That's [Z].

I can show you a cheaper number, but what changes is the middle or the last piece. The materials stay the same if we're doing the job right.

What I don't want you to do is hire someone who gives you a number that looks better on paper and ends up costing you more in two years.

I'm not telling you we're the cheapest option. I'm telling you we're the most predictable option."

*[If they still push back:]*

"Here's what I can do. Let me look at the scope again and see if there's anything we can phase or adjust without compromising the outcome. Can I have 24 hours to come back to you with options?"

---

*Notes: Don't discount reflexively. Discounting in response to "that's too high" trains customers to always say that. The goal is to understand whether this is a real objection (can't afford it, competing quote) or a negotiating tactic. The script above is for the latter. If it's a real affordability issue, see Sales Script 9.*

---

#### Sales 6: Price Objection: "I Got a Cheaper Quote"
*Use when: customer has a competing bid at a lower number.*

---

"How much lower are we talking?"

*[Get the number. Don't assume.]*

"Okay. I'm not going to tell you that quote is wrong without seeing it. Can I ask: did they break out materials, labor, and overhead as line items, or was it just a total number?"

*[Most competing quotes are totals.]*

"Here's why I ask. A total number tells you what you'll pay. It doesn't tell you what you're buying. The difference between our number and theirs usually comes down to one of three things: the scope of work is different, the materials spec is different, or they're running at a lower overhead because they're a smaller operation with fewer protections in place.

None of those are automatically bad. But they're worth knowing before you sign.

If you send me their estimate, I'll compare them line by line and tell you exactly where the difference is. If it's legitimate, same scope, same spec, same warranty, I'll tell you that too, and you should take the lower number. I'm not here to win a price war. I'm here to make sure you're comparing the same thing."

*[If they send it and the scopes differ:]*

"Here's what I found. They're not including [X]. That's not a flaw in their bid. They may have just scoped it differently. But if you get a year down the road and [X] fails, that becomes an out-of-pocket repair. Our bid covers it. You can decide if that protection is worth [price difference]."

---

*Notes: This script converts more often than any other because it takes the conversation from price to value. Most customers have never had a contractor offer to compare bids transparently. That move alone builds more trust than any discount. Use it every time.*

---

#### Sales 7: Price Objection: "I Need to Think About It"
*Use when: at the end of a presentation or estimate review, customer says they need time to think.*

---

"Of course. I don't want you to feel rushed.

Before I let you go: can I ask what specifically you want to think through? I want to make sure you have everything you need to make a good decision, and if there's something I haven't answered well, I'd rather address it now."

*[Wait. Most of the time the real objection comes out here.]*

*[If they surface a real concern:]*

"I'm glad you brought that up. Let me address it directly."

*[Handle the specific concern.]*

*[If they don't surface anything specific:]*

"Fair enough. Here's what I'll do: I'll follow up in two days to answer any questions that come up once you've had time to look everything over. Does that work?

And one thing I'd ask: if you do decide to go another direction, would you be willing to tell me why? I'm always trying to get better and the feedback is genuinely useful to me."

---

*Notes: "I need to think about it" is not an objection. It's a gap. Something in the presentation didn't land or a concern wasn't addressed. The question "what specifically do you want to think through" turns a vague delay into a real conversation. In most cases, if you handle the concern that surfaces, the deal closes on the same call.*

---

#### Sales 8: Price Objection: "Can You Match That Price?"
*Use when: customer asks directly if you'll drop to a competitor's number.*

---

"I appreciate you asking directly. I'll give you a direct answer.

I can't just match a number without knowing what that number is buying. What I can do is make sure we're comparing the same thing.

If the scope is identical and the spec is identical and the warranty is identical, I'll tell you honestly whether I can get close. I'm not going to lose a job over a small gap if everything else is equal.

But if they're giving you a number that doesn't include [X, Y, Z], then matching it means cutting those things out, and then you're not getting what we talked about. That's not something I'm willing to do because it would come back on you and on us."

*[If they push for a number:]*

"Give me the competing estimate and let me review it tonight. I'll come back to you tomorrow with a clear answer: either here's where we're comparable and here's my best number, or here's what's different and why the gap exists."

---

*Notes: Never match a number cold without seeing the competing scope. This script gives you 24 hours to review and come back with a principled answer. It also signals to the customer that you're a serious operator, not a price-auction participant. Most customers respect this more than an immediate discount.*

---

#### Sales 9: Price Objection: "I Don't Have the Money Right Now"
*Use when: the objection is genuine affordability, not negotiation.*

---

"I hear you. And I want to make sure I understand: are we talking about timing, like the cash isn't available for 30 or 60 days, or is this more of a budget constraint where this project doesn't fit what you can spend right now?"

*[They answer.]*

*[If it's timing:]*

"Okay. Here's what we can do. I can hold the estimate at today's pricing for 30 days and schedule the job for [date range]. That way you're not losing the quote while you get the cash in order. Does that help?"

*[If it's a budget constraint:]*

"I understand. Let me ask: is there a version of this project that you could do now that addresses the urgent issue and defers the rest? Sometimes we can phase the work so you're protected where it matters most without doing everything at once.

That's not me trying to find a way to get a smaller check from you. It's making sure the critical issue doesn't get worse while you figure out the budget."

*[If they truly cannot move forward:]*

"That's completely okay. Here's my card. When your situation changes, call me first. I'd rather give you a good experience when you're ready than push you into something that doesn't work for you right now."

---

*Notes: Genuine affordability objections require a completely different energy than negotiating objections. Pushing hard on a customer who actually can't pay damages your reputation and produces bad outcomes. Phase options, timing holds, and a graceful exit are the tools here. Customers who feel respected when they can't buy come back when they can.*

---

#### Sales 10: Trust Objection: "How Long Have You Been in Business?"
*Use when: customer asks about company age/history, usually a proxy for a trust concern.*

---

"[Company name] has been operating since [year]. I've personally been in this industry for [X] years.

But I want to give you a better answer than just a number, because honestly, time in business is only one part of what you're actually asking.

What you want to know is whether we'll be here after the job is done. Whether we'll answer the phone if something comes up. Whether the people doing the work know what they're doing.

Here's how I'd answer that: we've done [X] jobs in [city/region]. We have [X] reviews online and our average rating is [X]. I can give you references from jobs completed in the last 90 days, not 3 years ago.

And if something goes wrong on a job we did, I handle it personally. That's not a company policy. That's how I run my business.

Does that answer what you're really asking?"

---

*Notes: "How long have you been in business?" is almost never actually about years. It's about confidence that you'll stand behind the work. Answer the real question, not the surface question. If you're a newer company, lead with references and warranties, not the year you started.*

---

#### Sales 11: Timing Objection: "Let Me Wait Until Next Season"
*Use when: customer wants to defer, citing timing or season.*

---

"I understand. Timing matters for this kind of work.

Can I ask what's driving the decision to wait? Is it budget timing, scheduling on your end, or something else?"

*[They answer.]*

*[If it's budget:]*
"See Sales Script 9."

*[If it's a genuine preference to defer:]*

"Fair. Here's what I want you to think about before we close out today's conversation: the condition this [roof/property] is in right now is not going to hold at the same level for another season. I'm not saying that to pressure you. I'm saying it because part of my job is giving you an honest picture of the risk.

If [specific issue] gets worse over the next four to six months, the scope of work changes and the price goes up. I'd rather you make an informed decision now than come back in the spring and find out a $12,000 job turned into a $22,000 job.

If you still want to wait, I respect that. Can I schedule a check-in call for [date] so we can reassess? I'll keep your file open and your pricing held as close as I can at that point."

---

*Notes: The goal is not to overcome the timing objection by arguing. It's to make sure the customer has the information they need to make an informed decision about the risk of waiting. If they understand the risk and still want to wait, let them. Forced closes on timing objections produce the most cancellations and disputes.*

---

#### Sales 12: Spouse Objection: "I Need to Talk to My Spouse"
*Use when: customer is ready to move but defers to absent spouse or partner.*

---

"Of course. That's a smart way to handle a decision this size.

Two things I want to leave you with for that conversation.

First: here's a summary of what we looked at and what we found. I'll send this to you by email so you can share it directly. It has the scope, the pricing, and the warranty terms all in one place. It will save you from trying to explain it from memory.

Second: if your spouse has questions that I can answer better than you can, I'm happy to jump on a quick call with both of you. No pressure. Just want to make sure you both have the full picture.

When do you think you'll have that conversation?"

*[They give a timeline.]*

"Perfect. I'll follow up [day after their timeline]. And if anything comes up before then, here's my direct number."

---

*Notes: Never push back on the spouse objection. It is a legitimate decision-making dynamic in most households. The two moves that close more spouse-objection deals than anything else are: (1) providing a summary document they can share, and (2) offering a joint call. The joint call offer closes the deal more than half the time because the customer realizes they can just get the answer now.*

---

#### Sales 13: Insurance Objection: "I Don't Want to File a Claim"
*Use when: customer has legitimate insurance damage but is reluctant to use their coverage.*

---

"I hear that. It comes up a lot. Can I ask what's driving that concern: is it the deductible, the worry about rates going up, or something else?"

*[They answer.]*

*[If it's rate concern:]*

"That's a fair concern. Here's the reality: one claim on a homeowner's policy does not automatically increase your rates. That's a myth that gets repeated a lot. Your carrier looks at your claims history over time. One legitimate claim, especially for storm damage, is what the policy is for.

And here's the math: your deductible is probably $1,000 to $2,500. The damage we're looking at is [$X]. If you don't file, you pay [$X] out of pocket. If you file, you pay your deductible. That's the only trade-off you're actually making."

*[If it's deductible concern:]*

"What's your deductible?"

*[They tell you.]*

"Okay. So the question is whether [$deductible] is worth the protection on a [$X] scope of work. That's the actual decision. I'm not going to make that for you. But I want to make sure the number you're working with is the real number."

---

*Notes: The insurance objection is almost always based on incomplete information. Educating the customer, calmly and factually without pushing, closes more of these than any sales tactic. Do not make promises about rate impacts. State only what you know.*

---

#### Sales 14: Closing the Inspection
*Use when: at the end of the inspection walkthrough, before leaving the property.*

---

"Let me recap what I found and make sure we're on the same page.

[Summary of findings: 3 to 5 bullet points, specific, no jargon.]

Based on what I saw, here's what I recommend: [recommendation in plain language].

The way we typically move forward from here is: I get you an estimate within 24 hours, we review it together, and if it makes sense for both of us we schedule the work. The inspection today doesn't commit you to anything.

Before I leave: do you have any questions about what I found, or anything I didn't look at that you wanted me to see?"

*[Handle questions.]*

"One more thing: what does your decision process look like from here? Is this something you want to move on quickly, or are you going to take a few weeks to decide?"

*[This surfaces timeline, competing quotes, and spouse dynamics before you invest in writing an estimate.]*

"Got it. I'll have the estimate to you by [time]. If you don't hear from me by then, call me directly at [number]. I'll also follow up the morning after I send it to make sure you got it."

---

*Notes: The closing question "what does your decision process look like from here" is one of the most valuable questions in the entire sales process. It tells you: timeline, other bidders, decision authority, and motivation level. Ask it before you leave every single inspection.*

---

#### Sales 15: Closing the Proposal
*Use when: estimate has been sent, following up to move toward a signature.*

---

"Hi [name], this is [your name] from [company]. You got the estimate I sent over?"

*[They confirm.]*

"Good. I wanted to follow up personally rather than just waiting on email. Do you have 5 minutes to walk through it?"

*[If yes:]*

"The summary is [brief scope recap]. The total is [$X]. The timeline if you want to move forward this week would be [start date].

I want to ask you directly: is there anything in that estimate that gives you pause or that you want me to explain?"

*[Handle whatever comes up.]*

"Based on what you're telling me, it sounds like [summary of their situation]. Here's what I'd recommend: if you're ready to move forward, I can have a contract to you in the next hour and we can lock in the start date. If you need more time, tell me what you need from me to get there."

*[Ask for the close.]*

"So: are we moving forward on this?"

---

*Notes: Ask for the close explicitly. Most sales are lost not to objections but to salespeople who never asked. The direct question "are we moving forward on this?" creates a decision moment. The customer has to give you a yes, a no, or a specific objection. All three are better than continued follow-up limbo.*

---

#### Sales 16: Referral Ask
*Use when: job is complete, customer is satisfied. Ask in person or by phone, not text.*

---

"Before I leave, I want to ask you for a favor, and I want to be upfront about what I'm asking.

We do most of our business through referrals from customers who had a good experience. You've been a great customer and the job came out exactly the way it should.

If you have a neighbor, a family member, a coworker, anyone who has mentioned needing [type of work] or who you've been telling about the project. I'd really appreciate an introduction. Not just a name. An actual introduction, even a text message saying 'call these guys.' That carries a lot more weight than just passing along our number.

In return, if they become a customer, I'll send you a [referral gift: gift card, credit, etc.].

Is there anyone who comes to mind right now?"

*[Pause and wait for the answer. Don't fill the silence.]*

*[If they give a name:]*

"Perfect. Can you send them a text right now while I'm here? I want to make sure I'm reaching out to someone who's already expecting my call."

---

*Notes: The referral ask works best when done in person immediately after job completion. The request for a live introduction, not just a name, is the key detail. Names without introductions convert at a fraction of the rate of warm referrals. Ask for the text introduction on the spot.*

---

#### Sales 17: Google Review Ask
*Use when: job is complete, same visit or follow-up call within 48 hours.*

---

"One more quick thing before I let you go. We rely on Google reviews to help customers like you find us in the first place. It's basically how word of mouth works online now.

If you're willing to leave us a review, I'd really appreciate it. It doesn't have to be long. Even two or three sentences about your experience: what we did, whether we showed up on time, whether the job came out the way you expected. It makes a real difference for us.

I can text you the direct link right now so you don't have to hunt for it. Would that be okay?"

*[Send the Google review link via text immediately.]*

*[Follow-up if they don't leave a review within 5 days:]*

"Hi [name], following up on that Google review link I sent over. No pressure. I just wanted to make sure you got it. Here it is again if you want to take 2 minutes: [link]."

---

*Notes: Timing is everything on review requests. The highest conversion rate is within 24 hours of job completion. Send the link directly. Don't ask them to find you on Google. The follow-up message should be sent once and only once.*

### OPERATIONS SCRIPTS

#### Ops 1: Monday Morning Huddle
*Use weekly. 15 minutes maximum. Standing meeting. Same time every week.*

---

"Good morning. Let's make this quick. Here's where we are.

Last week: [revenue collected, jobs completed, any notable wins or problems: 2 sentences max].

This week: [jobs scheduled, crew assignments, key milestones: 1 sentence per active job].

One thing I need everyone to pay attention to this week: [safety item, process issue, customer situation, or operational priority: 1 specific thing only].

Any blockers I need to know about before we go? Anyone short on material, waiting on a permit, or dealing with a customer issue I don't know about?"

*[Take notes. Handle what can be handled in 2 minutes. Table the rest.]*

"That's it. Let's have a good week."

---

*Notes: The Monday huddle works only if it stays under 15 minutes. The moment it becomes a 45-minute meeting, attendance drops and the habit dies. One topic. One priority. Quick problem flags. Done. The purpose is alignment, not discussion.*

---

#### Ops 2: Weekly Numbers Review
*Use weekly with office manager or direct report. 20-30 minutes.*

---

"Let's go through the numbers. Walk me through last week."

*Items to cover in order:*

**Revenue:** "What did we invoice last week? What did we collect? What's the gap and what's in it?"

**Jobs in progress:** "How many active jobs are we running? What's the status on each: on track, behind, or flagged?"

**Accounts receivable:** "What's the total AR balance? Anything over 45 days? What's the oldest open invoice and have we contacted them?"

**Cash position:** "What's in the bank today? What do we have coming in this week? What do we have going out?"

**Pipeline:** "How many estimates are out? What's the total value? What's the next step on each one?"

---

"Okay. Based on all of that, what's the one thing I need to do this week that I'm not already doing?"

*[This question gets you out of reporting mode and into problem-solving mode.]*

---

*Notes: Run this meeting with the same list every week. Variation in the structure makes it easy to avoid uncomfortable numbers. The consistency forces you to confront what's changing. Most operators who run this meeting weekly for 90 days find one recurring problem they'd been ignoring.*

---

#### Ops 3: Safety Toolbox Talk
*Use at job site before work begins. 5-10 minutes. Rotate topics weekly.*

---

"Before we start today, I want to spend 5 minutes on [safety topic: fall protection / ladder safety / heat exposure / electrical hazards / tool safety / chemical handling / etc.].

Here's what I want you to think about today: [one specific hazard relevant to this job site or this week's work conditions].

The rule on this job site is: if you see something that isn't safe, you stop. You don't push through it. You don't let the schedule override the safety call. You come to me.

Any questions about today's work or anything you saw last week that concerned you?"

*[Pause. Actually wait for answers.]*

"Good. Let's get to work. [Any specific setup instructions for today's job.] First priority is [X]. Let's get that staged before we do anything else."

---

*Notes: Safety talks only work if they're specific and two-way. A foreman who reads from a laminated card while the crew stands around gets zero retention. Make it specific to the job site. Ask a real question. Wait for an answer. This takes 7 minutes and prevents OSHA incidents that cost $50K+.*

---

#### Ops 4: Job Kickoff Checklist

*Complete before crew begins any work. Foreman responsible.*

---

**Before Arriving on Site:**
- Work order confirmed and printed or saved to phone
- Materials ordered and confirmed for delivery date
- Permit pulled (if required) and on site or digital copy available
- Customer notified of start date and time
- Emergency contact for customer confirmed
- Crew headcount confirmed for this job

**On Arrival:**
- Introduce yourself to customer (or confirm they're not home and we have access)
- Walk the full scope before any work begins
- Identify any site hazards: overhead lines, fragile surfaces, drainage concerns
- Confirm dumpster or material staging area with customer if applicable
- Take pre-job photos: full perimeter, any pre-existing damage, any scope items noted in the work order

**Before First Tool Hits:**
- Safety briefing completed (Ops Script 3)
- Crew assignments given: who owns what section or task
- Material delivery confirmed on-site or ETA known
- Customer expectations confirmed: hours of work, parking, access to utilities

**End of Day:**
- Site secured, materials covered if needed
- Debris removed from customer's driveway and yard
- Customer updated on progress (in person or by text)
- Photo update sent to office
- Tomorrow's crew and start time confirmed

---

#### Ops 5: Job Completion Checklist

*Complete before final invoice is sent.*

---

**On Site:**
- Full scope walkthrough completed with foreman
- Punch list items identified and documented (see Ops Script 6)
- All debris removed from property
- Dumpster removed or scheduled
- Final photos taken: all completed work, full perimeter
- Customer walkthrough completed
- Customer signed off on completion (verbal confirmation minimum, written preferred)

**Back Office:**
- Final photos uploaded to job file
- Any scope changes documented and priced
- Final invoice generated with correct amounts
- Invoice sent to customer with payment instructions
- Warranty documentation sent to customer
- Google review ask sent (Sales Script 17)
- Referral ask completed (Sales Script 16)
- Job file closed in CRM

**30-Day Follow-Up:**
- Scheduled call or text to confirm no issues
- Any warranty items identified and scheduled

#### Ops 6: Punch List Checklist

*Use when: any incomplete or unsatisfactory item is identified at job end.*

---

*For each punch list item, document:*

1. Description of item: what is incomplete or incorrect
2. Who is responsible for the fix
3. What materials or resources are needed
4. Target completion date
5. Customer aware: yes/no

*Template line:*
> Item: [description] | Responsible: [name] | Materials needed: [yes/no and what] | Completion date: [date] | Customer notified: [yes/no]

---

*Notes: A punch list with no names and no dates is a list of things that won't get done. Every item needs an owner and a deadline. The foreman closes the punch list, not the owner.*

#### Ops 7: Vendor Renegotiation Email

*Use when: renegotiating pricing or terms with a material supplier or subcontractor. Send before any in-person conversation.*

---

Subject: Pricing review, [Company Name] account

[Vendor contact name],

I want to set up a conversation about our current pricing arrangement. We've been doing business together for [X years/months] and we've placed approximately [$X] in orders over that period.

I'm reviewing all of our vendor relationships this quarter as part of a cost structure review. I'd like to understand what pricing options are available to us given our current volume, and whether there's a structure that makes sense for both of us going forward.

I'm not here to pressure you. I'm looking to have an honest conversation. If our volume justifies a better rate, I'd like to know what that looks like. If it doesn't, I'd like to understand what volume threshold would get us there.

I'll have someone reach out to schedule a call. I'd like to get this done in the next two weeks.

[Your name]
[Company]

---

*Notes: This email does two things: it signals that you're serious (you've done a cost review, you have a timeline) and it frames the conversation as mutual rather than adversarial. Do not lead with "I need lower prices." Lead with volume history and a question about structure.*

---

#### Ops 8: Vendor Renegotiation: In-Person Script
*Use in the actual meeting after the email above.*

---

"Thanks for making time. I'll get right to it.

We're doing approximately [$X] per [month/year] with you right now. Based on our projections for this year, that number is going to be [higher/similar/growing as we scale].

I did a competitive review. I'm not going to pretend I didn't. I found [X: pricing from a competitor, different structure, etc.]. I didn't come in here to threaten you with that. I came in here because you've been a reliable supplier and I'd rather figure out a structure that keeps us working together.

Here's what I need: [specific ask: X% reduction on [material], net 30 terms instead of net 15, volume pricing above $X per month, etc.].

What can you do?"

*[Stop talking. Wait for their response.]*

*[If they push back:]*

"I understand you have your own constraints. Here's what I'm working with: if I can't get [X], the math doesn't work for me to keep routing this volume through you exclusively. I'm not bluffing. I'm telling you so you have the information.

What would you need from me to make this work on your end?"

---

*Notes: Vendors negotiate every day. You should too. The operators who never renegotiate are subsidizing the ones who do. Bring your purchase history to this meeting in writing. Numbers are more persuasive than conversations.*

---

#### Ops 9: Accounts Receivable: 30-Day Collection Call
*Use when: invoice is 30 days past due. First outreach.*

---

"Hi [customer name], this is [your name] from [company]. I'm calling about invoice number [X] for the work we completed at [address] on [date]. The total is [$X] and it was due on [date].

I want to make sure nothing fell through the cracks on your end. Did you receive the invoice?"

*[If they received it:]*

"Okay. Is there anything that needs to be resolved before you're able to process payment?"

*[Handle whatever comes up.]*

"What date can I expect payment? I want to make a note in our system so we're not following up unnecessarily."

*[Get a specific date.]*

---

#### Ops 10: Accounts Receivable: 60-Day Collection Call
*Use when: invoice is 60 days past due and earlier outreach has not resulted in payment.*

---

"Hi [name], this is [your name] from [company]. I'm following up on invoice [X] for [$X], which is now 60 days past due. We've reached out previously and I want to resolve this today.

I understand things come up. Can you tell me what's going on with this invoice?"

*[Listen. Don't argue.]*

*[If they have a dispute:]*

"I wasn't aware of that. Tell me more."

*[Handle the dispute. If it's legitimate, address it. If it's not:]*

"I hear you. Based on the signed contract and the completion documentation, we fulfilled the scope as agreed. Here's what I'd like to do: let's set up a time this week to look at this together. I want to resolve it, not escalate it. Can you do [day/time]?"

*[If no dispute, just non-payment:]*

"I need a payment commitment today. What can you do and by when?"

---

#### Ops 11: Accounts Receivable: 90-Day Collection Call
*Use when: invoice is 90+ days past due. This call sets up escalation if no resolution.*

---

"[Name], this is [your name] from [company]. Invoice [X] for [$X] is now 90 days past due. We've spoken about this before.

I need to be direct with you: at 90 days, this goes to our collections process. I don't want to do that. It creates problems for both of us. But I also can't carry a [$X] receivable indefinitely.

Here's what I'm willing to do to resolve this today: [payment plan terms, for example: $X now and $X in 30 days]. That's the last option before we escalate.

Can you do that?"

*[If yes: confirm in writing immediately.]*
*[If no or non-responsive: document the call and send to collections or small claims process.]*

---

*Notes: 90-day receivables have a significantly lower collection rate than 30-day receivables. Prevention is the best strategy: get a draw schedule in the contract, collect a deposit before material orders, and send invoices the day the job closes. Every day you wait after 30 days, the probability of full collection drops.*

#### Ops 12: Customer Complaint Resolution Script
*Use when: customer calls with a complaint about work quality, timing, crew behavior, or billing.*

---

"I'm glad you called. Tell me what happened."

*[Listen completely. Don't interrupt. Don't defend. Don't explain yet.]*

*[After they finish:]*

"Thank you for being direct with me. I want to understand this fully. Can I ask a few questions?"

*[Ask clarifying questions: when did they first notice the issue, has it gotten worse, who on our crew did they speak to, etc.]*

"Here's where I stand: [one of three scenarios below]"

*[Scenario 1: legitimate issue on our side:]*

"You're right. That's not acceptable and I'm going to fix it. Here's what I'm going to do: [specific action, specific timeline]. I'll personally follow up with you when it's done."

*[Scenario 2: unclear ownership of the issue:]*

"I need to look into this before I can give you a definitive answer. I'm going to review the job file and talk to the crew by [date] and call you back. I'm not brushing you off. I need the facts before I respond."

*[Scenario 3: issue not related to our scope:]*

"I understand why you're frustrated. After reviewing what we did against what's happening now, here's what I found: [explanation]. I'm not saying this to avoid responsibility. I'm saying it because I want to be straight with you. Here's what I can offer: [fair resolution, even if it's just an explanation]."

---

#### Ops 13: Warranty Callback Script
*Use when: customer calls about a warranty issue after job completion.*

---

"Hi [name], thanks for calling. Tell me what you're seeing."

*[They describe the issue.]*

"Okay. And when did you first notice this?"

*[Get timeline.]*

"Here's what I want to do: I want to get someone out there to look at it in person before I tell you what we're dealing with. When's the earliest you have availability?"

*[Schedule the visit.]*

"I'll be honest with you: once we look at it, there are a few possible explanations. It could be something covered under warranty, in which case we handle it at no cost to you. It could be something outside the scope of the warranty, in which case I'll explain exactly why and what your options are. Either way, you'll know what you're dealing with before you hang up with me on that call."

*[After the site visit:]*

"Here's what we found: [specific explanation]. Based on our warranty terms, [covered/not covered] because [specific reason]. Here's what we're going to do: [action and timeline]."

---

#### Ops 14: Insurance Adjuster Objection Script
*Use when: adjuster's scope misses legitimate items or undervalues the claim.*

---

"I appreciate you going through the scope with me. I want to flag a few items before we close this out.

On [line item], the scope shows [X]. Based on the damage we documented and the current replacement cost for this market, that number is [X% below market / missing the following items / not reflective of code requirements in this jurisdiction].

I have photos, measurements, and current supplier pricing to support each of these items. I'm not here to inflate the scope. I'm here to make sure we agree on what the actual scope is.

Can we go through these line items together?"

*[If adjuster is resistant:]*

"I understand you're working within your guidelines. What's the process for escalating a scope dispute? I want to work within the right channel, not create a problem for you."

*[Document everything. Follow up in writing.]*

"After our conversation today, I'll send you a summary of the items in question with supporting documentation. If there's a formal supplement process, I'll use it."

---

*Use when: submitting a supplement request to an insurance adjuster after initial scope approval.*

---

Subject: Supplement Request, Claim #[XXXXX], [Address]

[Adjuster Name],

Following our initial scope agreement on [date], we identified additional items during the course of work that were not included in the original scope. I'm submitting this supplement for your review.

**Supplement Items:**

Quantity: [X]
Unit: [SQ / LF / EA]
Unit Price: [X]
Total: [$X]
Justification: [Specific reason: code requirement, discovered damage, scope change, etc.]
Supporting documentation: [photos / measurements / code citation attached]

Item 2: [same format]

**Total Supplement Request:** [$X]

All items above are supported by the attached documentation. I'm available to review any of these by phone or to provide additional documentation if needed. My direct number is [phone].

### LEADERSHIP SCRIPTS

---

#### Leadership 1: 1-on-1 Meeting Agenda
*Use weekly or bi-weekly with any direct report. 20-30 minutes. Consistent structure.*

---

**Open (2 min):**
"How are you doing? Anything I need to know before we get into it?"

**Their update first (10 min):**
"Tell me what you're working on. What's moving, what's stuck, and what do you need from me?"

*[Take notes. Resist the urge to jump to solutions. Listen for patterns.]*

**Your update (5 min):**
"Here's what's coming from my side that affects you: [context on company priorities, changes, resource decisions]."

**One hard question (5 min):**
Choose one from the list below, rotate weekly:
- "What's one thing I'm doing that's making your job harder?"
- "If you could change one thing about how we operate, what would it be?"
- "What decision are you holding off on that you should just make?"
- "Where are you most likely to drop the ball in the next two weeks?"
- "What do you need to know more about to do your job better?"

**Close (2 min):**
"What are your top three priorities before we meet again? And is there anything you need from me to hit them?"

---

*Notes: The 1-on-1 is not a status meeting. Status can go in writing. The 1-on-1 is the place where you learn what your direct report actually thinks, what's actually slowing them down, and what they need to grow. The hard question is the only question that can't be answered by email. Ask it every time.*

---

#### Leadership 2: Conflict Mediation Script
*Use when: two team members have a conflict that is affecting work. Meet with both together.*

---

"I asked both of you in here because something is affecting how we're working, and I'd rather deal with it directly than let it get worse. I'm not here to decide who's right. I'm here to make sure we can work together.

[Name A], I want to hear your side first without interruption. Then [Name B] will have the same time. Then we talk together. Is that fair?"

*[Name A speaks. Name B does not interrupt. You take notes.]*
*[Name B speaks. Name A does not interrupt. You take notes.]*

"Here's what I heard from both of you: [brief synthesis: not who's right, just what the actual friction points are].

Here's what I need from this conversation: [specific behavioral agreement between the two: not feelings, not who's to blame, what changes going forward].

I'm going to ask each of you to name one specific thing you will do differently. [Name A] first."

*[Both name a concrete change.]*

"Good. I'm going to follow up with both of you in one week to see if things have shifted. If they haven't, we'll have a different conversation."

---

*Notes: Most workplace conflict is not actually about the presenting issue. It's about respect, credit, or unclear ownership of responsibility. The synthesis step, repeating back what you heard without taking sides, defuses the adversarial dynamic. Your job in this meeting is to get to a behavioral agreement, not to adjudicate who's right.*

---

#### Leadership 3: Praise Script
*Use when: a team member did something that deserves recognition. Do it in person, not just via text.*

---

"I want to tell you something directly.

[Specific situation: the job, the decision, the customer call, the problem they solved.]

What you did there was [specific description of the behavior, not the result]. That's not small. That's the kind of thing that's hard to teach and you did it without being asked.

I wanted to tell you in person because I don't want that to go unrecognized."

*[Stop there. Don't immediately follow with a "but" or a redirect to something else they need to improve. Let the recognition land.]*

---

*Notes: Praise is underused in most small businesses because owners are always focused on what's broken. Specific, in-person recognition tied to a particular behavior is 10 times more valuable than a general "great job" or a group text. People repeat what gets recognized. Be precise about what you're recognizing.*

---

#### Leadership 4: Difficult Feedback Script
*Use when: a direct report has a performance or behavior issue that needs to be addressed directly.*

---

"I want to have a direct conversation with you. I'm going to tell you something that's hard to hear, and I need you to actually hear it, not just wait for me to finish.

[Specific behavior or performance issue: not general, not vague. "In the last three weeks, you've missed two crew start times and didn't communicate ahead of time either time." Not "you've been showing up late."]*

Here's the impact: [specific effect on the business, the team, or the customer. Numbers if possible.]

I'm not questioning your effort overall. I'm telling you this specific thing is not acceptable at the level we're running at.

Here's what I need to see change: [specific, observable behavior. "Starting at the scheduled time or notifying the office at minimum 30 minutes before if there's a delay."]

I'm going to check in on this in two weeks. If it's changed, we won't need to talk about it again. If it hasn't, we'll need to talk about whether this role is the right fit.

Any questions?"

---

*Notes: Difficult feedback that's too vague does nothing. "I need you to step it up" is not feedback. "You missed two start times without notice in the last three weeks and it cost us two hours of crew time both days" is feedback. Name the behavior. Name the impact. Name the expectation. Give a timeline.*

---

#### Leadership 5: Promotion Announcement
*Use when: promoting someone from within. Tell them in a 1-on-1 first, then announce to the team.*

---

**The 1-on-1 announcement:**

"I want to talk to you about something before it goes to the rest of the team.

I've been watching how you've operated over the last [X months]. [Specific behavior or decision that demonstrated readiness.] That's not the kind of thing everyone can do.

I'm offering you [new title]. Here's what that means: [specific responsibilities, scope change, who they'll manage if applicable, comp change].

This is a real change in what I'm asking of you. I want to make sure you want it before we make it official. Do you?"

*[Wait for their answer. Give them space to ask questions or ask for time.]*

---

**The team announcement:**

"I want to tell everyone something that's effective immediately.

[Name] is moving into the role of [title]. This is not a surprise to anyone who's been paying attention. [One specific reason: describe a behavior or result, not just tenure.]

[Name], I'm glad you're in this role. The team is better for it."

---

#### Leadership 6: New Hire Announcement
*Use at a team meeting when introducing a new hire.*

---

"I want to introduce someone who's joining us. This is [name].

[Name] is coming in as [role]. Before this, they [brief relevant background: one sentence, not a full resume].

We brought [him/her/them] in because [specific reason tied to a real business need: "we needed someone who could own production coordination so I can stop doing it" is better than "we're growing"].

[Name], tell people one thing about yourself that has nothing to do with work."

*[This breaks the ice and humanizes the introduction.]*

"That's it. Welcome. [Name] starts [day]. If you need anything from them or they need anything from you, figure it out directly."

---

#### Leadership 7: Letting Go of a Long-Tenured Employee
*Use when: someone who has been with you for years needs to leave. This is the hardest script.*

---

"I've been thinking about how to have this conversation for a while. I owe you honesty.

You've been here for [X years] and that matters. You were here when [reference to a real moment: early days, a hard job, a growth moment]. I don't take that lightly.

What I have to tell you is that the role you're in and the business we're running have moved in different directions. [Specific: the business needs X, and what we've built together isn't getting us there anymore.] I've been avoiding this conversation, and that wasn't fair to you either.

Effective [date], we're going to end your employment. Here's what that looks like: [severance if applicable, transition period, reference, etc.].

I want to handle this with the respect you deserve for what you've put into this. I'm going to be straight with you and I'd ask you to be straight with me if you have questions."

---

*Notes: Long-tenured employee separations are handled badly more often than any other difficult conversation. Avoid the instinct to soften it so much that the message doesn't land. They deserve directness. Reference their real contributions. Name the real business reason. Be specific about logistics. Don't rush through it.*

---

#### Leadership 8: Company-Wide Email: Tough Month
*Use when: a month came in significantly below plan. Send to the full team.*

---

Subject: Where we're at, [Month] recap

Team,

I want to give you an honest picture of last month.

We came in at [$X] in revenue against a target of [$X]. Gross margin was [X%], which was [X points] below where we need to be. Cash position at month end was [$X].

Here's what drove it: [2 to 3 specific causes. Not blame. Not excuses. Facts.]

Here's what we're doing about it: [specific actions with owners and timelines].

I'm not writing this to create panic. I'm writing it because you're part of this business and you deserve to know where we stand. The operators who close information gaps are the ones who build the team's trust. I'd rather you hear this from me than guess.

[Month] starts now. Here's what I need from everyone: [one specific ask: focus on X, communicate problems early, hit Y target].

Any questions, come to me directly.

[Your name]

---

#### Leadership 9: Company-Wide Email: Strong Month
*Use when: a month significantly exceeded plan. Send to the full team.*

---

Subject: [Month] recap, good one

Team,

[Month] came in at [$X] in revenue. That's [X% over / $X above] our target. Gross margin held at [X%]. Cash is in the best position it's been in [timeframe].

A few things that made this happen: [specific, attributed if possible. "The crew on the [job name] job completed under budget and on time." "The sales team closed [X] out of [X] estimates." "The office handled scheduling for 14 jobs in one week without a call going to voicemail."]

This is not a fluke. This is what we look like when we execute. I want everyone to understand that.

We're not done. [Brief look at what's next: target for next month, a hire we're making, a system we're building.] But today I want to make sure this month gets recognized for what it was.

[Your name]

---

#### Leadership 10: "I Need Help" Script
*Use when: you're stuck on a problem and need your team's honest input.*

---

*Call a team meeting or have a direct 1-on-1. This script works in either setting.*

---

"I want to be direct with you about something.

I've been dealing with [specific problem: not vague, not "things are hard." Example: "We've had three crew turnover events in 90 days and I don't fully understand why."] I've been trying to solve this on my own and I haven't cracked it.

I'm asking for your honest input. I don't need reassurance. I need the real picture from someone closer to it than I am.

Here's what I'm seeing: [brief summary of what you know].

Here's what I don't know: [specific gap: "I don't know if this is a comp issue, a culture issue, or a management issue on my part."]

What are you seeing that I'm not?"

*[Stop talking. Wait. This silence is important. If you fill it and you'll get polished answers instead of honest ones.]*

*[After they respond:]*

"That's useful. I'm going to think about what you said. And I want to be clear: asking you this is not a sign that I don't have a handle on the business. It's a sign that I know I can get a better answer by asking than by guessing."

---

*Notes: Owners who can say "I don't know, tell me what you're seeing" build significantly more loyal teams than owners who project false certainty. The first time you do this it will feel uncomfortable. The second time it will feel like leadership.*

## SECTION 5: Q&A ARCHIVE

*30 questions from $1M-$5M contractor operators with full answers. Sourced from real operator conversations.*

**Q1: I'm doing $2.3M in revenue but I barely have any cash. What's wrong?**

This is the most common question in this room, and the answer is almost always the same three things in combination.

First, your gross margin is lower than you think. Most operators at $2M-$3M are running 32-38% gross margin and believe they're at 40%+. The difference is unbilled change orders, material overruns they absorb, and labor hours that go over estimate. Until you job-cost everything, you don't know your real margin.

Second, your draw schedule isn't aligned with your cost exposure. If you're collecting 50% on completion but you're spending 70% of job costs before completion, you're floating 20% of every job. On $2.3M of revenue, that's $460K of cash float sitting in work-in-progress.

Third, your overhead has grown with your revenue but your pricing hasn't kept up. Every time you add a truck, a salary, an office, or an insurance policy without adjusting your prices, your overhead percentage climbs. If overhead is 18% of revenue and gross margin is 36%, your net is 18%. If overhead grows to 24%, you're at 12% net, on the same revenue with the same prices.

Run the Overhead Calculator. Then run the Job Costing Worksheet on your last 10 jobs. The gap between what you thought you made and what you actually made is where your cash went.

---

**Q2: My close rate dropped from 42% to 27% in the last 90 days. I haven't changed anything. What happened?**

When close rate drops without a visible cause, it's almost always one of four things.

The first is lead quality. If your lead source changed, such as a different Google ad campaign, a canvassing territory, a referral partner who slowed down. The new leads may have lower intent or lower qualification. Your 42% close rate may have been built on warmer leads than what's coming in now.

The second is follow-up timing. If your average time from estimate to follow-up increased by even 24-48 hours, close rate drops materially. Most signed contracts happen within 72 hours of the estimate being sent. After that, you're competing with inertia and other bids.

The third is pricing. If your prices went up and you didn't adjust your sales approach, you're presenting a higher number to the same customer with the same script. Price increases require a parallel conversation about value.

The fourth is seasonality. Close rates shift with season in most trades. A close rate comparison that spans a seasonal inflection point isn't apples to apples.

Run the Sales Conversion Tracker on your last 50 leads, split between the high-close period and the low-close period. Look for the stage where the drop is concentrated. That will tell you whether it's a lead quality problem, a follow-up problem, or a close problem.

---

**Q3: How do I know if I'm ready to hire my first salesperson?**

You're ready when you have more leads than you can run yourself, your close rate is above 35%, and you can document what your sales process looks like step by step.

That last one is the one most operators skip. If you can't write down what you do from first contact to signed contract, you can't hand that process to someone else. You'll just be giving a new hire a phone and hoping they figure it out.

The sequence is: document your process first. Then hire the person to run it. If you hire before the process exists, you're not hiring a salesperson. You're hiring someone to invent a process while they're supposed to be closing jobs.

Also: your first sales hire's close rate will be 60-70% of yours for the first 60 days. Plan for that. At Roof King, the office manager I hired hit 68% of my close rate in the first 30 days and 84% by day 90. The only reason she got there fast was that we had a documented process she could learn from.

---

**Q4: My crew foreman has been with me for four years and he's not growing with the company. What do I do?**

This is a loyalty vs. capability problem and it doesn't get easier by waiting.

The first thing to do is have a direct conversation with him about what the role needs to look like now versus when he started. Most long-tenured employees have never been told explicitly what the expectations are at the current stage of the business. They're operating on the expectations from year one. That conversation alone sometimes closes the gap.

If you have the conversation and the gap doesn't close within 90 days, you have a different decision to make. Loyalty is real and matters. But keeping someone in a role they can't execute is not fair to them or to you. It signals to your whole team that performance doesn't determine outcomes.

The way to handle it with respect is: be honest about the gap, give a clear and specific runway for change, and if it doesn't change, move them to a role that matches their actual capability or separate with the respect they've earned. Long-tenured employees deserve a harder conversation, not an easier one.

---

**Q5: I've raised my prices twice in the last year and my close rate hasn't dropped. Should I raise them again?**

Yes. The fact that your close rate hasn't dropped after two increases means the market is accepting your prices and you're not at your ceiling yet.

The practical test is: when you're the only bidder on a job, does the customer sign without a negotiation? If yes, you're almost certainly underpriced. When customers negotiate only when they have a competing bid, not on your price alone, you've found closer to the real ceiling.

Raise prices incrementally. 5-8% increases are usually below the threshold where customers notice. Test one specific service line first. Track the close rate on that line specifically for 60 days. If close rate holds, roll the increase to the full book.

One warning: price increases only work if your overhead is in order. Raising prices to cover overhead growth that you haven't controlled is a short-term fix. Get your overhead right first, then price to margin, then let prices reflect market conditions.

**Q6: How do I get my team to actually use our process instead of doing it their own way?**

The short answer is: the process has to be easier to follow than to ignore.

Most processes don't get followed because they were designed by the owner, written in a binder, and announced at a meeting. That's not implementation. That's documentation.

The process actually gets used when: (1) it's shorter than the workaround, (2) the consequences of not following it are visible and fast, and (3) the person who designed it follows it themselves.

Start with one process, not all of them. Pick the one that's failing most visibly. Make it as short as possible: a checklist, not a manual. Train one person on it, not the whole team. Get that person to use it consistently for 30 days. Let the result speak. Then expand.

The other thing that works: tie the process to a number that the team cares about. If your job kickoff checklist reduces the number of callback visits (which eat crew time), the crew has a reason to use it that isn't "because I said so."

**Q7: What's the right gross margin to target at $2M in revenue?**

The answer depends on your overhead structure, but as a general target for a $2M trades contractor: 40-45% gross margin is where you want to be.

Here's the math on why. If your overhead is 20-25% of revenue at $2M (which is typical if you have a small office, a couple of trucks, some admin), you need 40% gross margin to generate a 15-20% net margin. At 35% gross, you're at 10-15% net. At 30%, you're barely covering overhead.

The important thing is not the number itself. It's that you actually know what your gross margin is on a per-job basis. Most operators at $2M are averaging their margin across jobs and don't know that their best jobs are at 48% and their worst are at 22%. The average looks okay. The variance is the problem.

Job-cost everything. Find the jobs that are destroying your margin. Understand why. That will tell you more than any target number.

**Q8: I have $180K in accounts receivable and $22K in the bank. Is that normal?**

It's common. It's not fine.

$180K in AR against $22K in the bank means you've done the work, sent the invoice, but haven't been paid. The work cost you money. The cash hasn't come back yet. That gap is your float, and if it's structural, it will eventually cause a cash flow crisis.

The fix is two-part. Short term: call every invoice over 30 days this week. Prioritize by dollar amount. Get payment commitments with dates. For any invoice over 60 days, offer a payment plan if needed. You want cash, not a clean aging report.

Long term: restructure your draw schedules so you're collecting closer to your cost exposure. A job that costs you $15,000 in materials and labor over two weeks should not have the final payment due 30 days after completion. Collect a deposit. Collect progress payments. Collect more of the balance before the final invoice.

The goal is to get your AR balance to 3-4 weeks of revenue or less. At $2M annual run rate, that's $115-$150K. You're not far off. The discipline is the hard part.

**Q9: My best employee told me he's thinking about starting his own company. What do I do?**

Don't react to it as a threat. Respond to it as information.

The first conversation is about understanding why. Is he leaving because he doesn't see a future for himself in your company? Because he thinks he can make more money on his own? Because he's frustrated with something specific? The answer changes what you do.

If it's about trajectory: have a real conversation about what the next 12 months look like for him inside your company. If there's a path to more autonomy, more income, or more ownership, and you haven't communicated it, that's a fixable problem.

If it's about money: can you build a comp structure that gives him what ownership would give him without the risk? Profit sharing, performance bonuses tied to division performance, or a stake in a specific vertical are all options.

If he genuinely wants to build something for himself, you probably can't stop that, and trying too hard to stop it will create resentment and he'll leave anyway. The better play is to transition the relationship into something that benefits both of you: a referral arrangement, a subcontracting relationship, or just a clean and respectful exit that preserves the relationship.

The worst outcome is losing him and losing the relationship. The second worst is losing him poorly.

**Q10: How do I handle a customer who is threatening a bad review to get free work?**

Calmly and directly.

"I understand you're frustrated. I'd like to address any legitimate issue with the work. Can you tell me specifically what you feel was not completed as agreed?"

If there's a real issue, fix it. If it's being used as leverage: "I'm going to need to see the specific issue in writing so I can document it and address it properly. I'm not able to offer a credit or discount based on a review threat. I'd be happy to address any real deficiency in the work."

Do not offer a credit in exchange for not leaving a review. That is essentially bribery and it also trains customers to use this tactic again. It also doesn't work. People who use this tactic often leave the bad review anyway.

Document the conversation in writing. Respond to any negative review publicly and professionally: "We completed [scope] on [date] as agreed. We welcome the opportunity to address any specific deficiency in the work directly." Keep it factual, keep it brief. A professional response to a bad review often converts readers better than no review at all.

**Q11: What's the best way to structure a commission plan for a salesperson?**

Keep it simple and tied directly to what you actually want.

The structure that works well for most trades businesses at this revenue level: a modest base or draw (enough to cover living, not enough to remove hunger), plus a percentage of collected revenue on closed jobs. The percentage varies by industry and margin, but 5-8% of collected revenue on jobs above a minimum margin threshold is a common structure.

The key details that most commission plans get wrong:

First, tie it to collected revenue, not invoiced revenue. You don't want your salesperson chasing low-quality customers and then disappearing when the invoice doesn't get paid.

Second, include a margin floor. If a salesperson discounts a job below your minimum margin, they earn a lower commission rate or no commission on that job. This prevents chasing volume at the expense of profitability.

Third, make it legible. A rep should be able to calculate their commission on a napkin. Complex tiered structures confuse people and remove the behavioral incentive.

Test the math at three volumes: slow month, average month, strong month. At all three levels, the comp should feel fair to the rep and to you.

**Q12: When should I stop running jobs myself and manage full-time?**

When the cost of you being on the tools is higher than the cost of replacing yourself on the tools.

At $1M in revenue, the owner running jobs is often necessary. At $2M-$2.5M, you're usually at the transition point. At $3M+, if you're still the primary production driver, you have a structural problem.

The test is simple: what does your revenue per hour look like when you're on a job versus when you're in the office? Most operators find that an hour in the office working on sales, oversight, and systems is worth $200-$500 in business outcomes. An hour on the tools is worth $50-$80.

The practical move: hire one level below your current tools role before you make the full transition. Don't go from full production to full management overnight. Add a foreman or lead, train them on your standards, and slowly pull yourself out. Track the outcome for 90 days. If the work holds, pull back more.

The mental shift is harder than the logistics. Most owners who love the tools resist this transition longer than they should.

**Q13: My bookkeeper told me I made $180K last year but I can't find it in my bank account. What happened?**

This is an accrual vs. cash accounting problem, a retained earnings problem, or both.

Accrual accounting shows revenue when it's invoiced, not when it's paid. If $180K was the net income on accrual basis, your actual cash generation might have been $60K if you built up AR, increased inventory, or bought equipment.

The retained earnings issue is different: you made $180K, but you also paid yourself more than that in distributions. Many operators take draws throughout the year that far exceed their reported profit, then are confused about where the money went. Your bookkeeper can show you the distribution total for the year.

The third possibility: job costing is inaccurate and the $180K is overstated because costs aren't hitting the right period.

Have your bookkeeper produce a cash flow statement, not just a P&L. The cash flow statement will show you exactly where the cash went: operating activities, investing activities (equipment, vehicles), and financing activities (loans taken or repaid, distributions). That document tells the real story.

**Q14: How do I deal with a key employee who suddenly starts showing up late, seems checked out, and is having conflict with other team members?**

Talk to them before you diagnose it.

The behavioral pattern you're describing (disengagement, tardiness, interpersonal conflict) can come from three places: something happening in their personal life, something happening at work they haven't told you, or a fundamental shift in their engagement with the role.

Before you decide what to do, have a direct, private conversation: "I've noticed some things over the last few weeks and I want to check in with you directly. What's going on?"

If something personal is driving it, that doesn't change the performance expectation but it changes how you hold the accountability conversation. Knowing what you're dealing with lets you respond instead of react.

If something at work is driving it: a conflict with another employee, a change in their role, a perception of being passed over. That's often fixable if you know about it.

If it's disengagement with the role itself, that's the hardest conversation. People who are fundamentally done with a job rarely turn it around. The honest conversation in that case is: "It looks like this role isn't working for you anymore. What would need to change, and is that change something we can actually make?"

**Q15: I have three people who could potentially be promoted to production manager but I don't know how to choose.**

Run a structured evaluation, not a gut-feel decision.

Write down 5-7 specific criteria for the production manager role: crew management, job cost accuracy, customer communication, problem-solving under pressure, ability to operate independently, etc. Weight them by importance.

Score each of the three against those criteria based on what you've observed, not what you hope. Talk to other people on the team about each candidate: not who they'd pick, but what they see when each of the three are in a tough situation.

The thing most operators get wrong here is weighting loyalty and tenure too heavily. The best production manager is the one who can run the production system, not the one who's been there longest or who you like the most.

Be transparent with all three that you're making a decision and that you'll be communicating it directly. The two who don't get the role deserve an honest conversation about why: what the decision was based on and what they'd need to develop to be in a different position next time.

**Q16: We have a consistent problem with jobs going over on material. I've tried tracking it but the team ignores the system. What do I do?**

The team ignores it because there's no consequence and no feedback loop.

The current system probably works like this: over-materials happen, you find out at job close, you're frustrated, nothing changes. The person who ordered the extra materials never hears about it. It happens again.

The fix requires two things: (1) visibility at the right time, not after the fact, and (2) a person whose job it is to care.

On visibility: the foreman should be comparing actual material usage to estimated usage at the midpoint of every job, not at job close. If the job is halfway done and materials are already at 75% of budget, there's time to intervene. At job close, it's already too late.

On ownership: the foreman's performance review and any performance bonus should include a job cost accuracy metric. When someone's income is connected to material overruns, they start caring about material overruns. This is not punitive. It's alignment.

The specific metric: track material cost as a percentage of estimated material cost per job. Your foremen should be within 5% on average. If someone is consistently at 15-20% over, that's a training problem or a performance problem. The system can't tell you which. That conversation with the foreman can.

---

**Q17: How much should I pay myself?**

Enough to live on, not so much that you're hiding the business's real profitability from yourself.

At $1M-$2M in revenue, a reasonable owner salary is $80K-$120K, plus distributions when the business generates profit above operating needs. The salary should reflect what you'd have to pay someone to replace your management function, not what you want to make.

The mistake most operators make at this stage: they take draws throughout the year based on cash flow, don't formalize it as salary, and then look at a P&L that shows 22% net margin and think the business is doing great. Strip out the distributions and add back a market-rate salary for your role, and the real margin picture often looks very different.

The other mistake: paying yourself too little and subsidizing the business by not taking a real wage. This creates a false picture of profitability in the other direction. If the business can only run at these margins because the owner works for $40K, it's not a healthy business. It's a job.

Set a salary. Pay it like a bill. Take distributions on top of it when there's real profit to distribute.

**Q18: I keep hiring the wrong people. What's wrong with my process?**

Usually one of three things.

First: you're hiring for availability instead of fit. When a position is open and you're overwhelmed, you hire the first acceptable candidate instead of the right one. The urgency is real, but a bad hire costs you more than a 30-day wait for the right one.

Second: you're not screening for the right things. Most small business interviews are a conversation about work history. They should be a conversation about how the person handles specific situations. The scenario-based question format (describe a time when X happened: what did you do?) surfaces behavior instead of claims.

Third: you're not checking references on the things that matter. A reference check that asks "was this person a good employee?" gets a yes almost every time. A reference check that asks "tell me about the last time this person had to handle a conflict with a coworker" gets real information.

Also, look at your job descriptions. If they're vague, like "team player, self-starter, good communicator", they will attractt candidates who are good at sounding like those things, not candidates who actually are those things. Write job descriptions that describe specific behaviors and outcomes. Screen for those specifically.

---

**Q19: My insurance costs went up 40% this year. How do I handle this without killing my margin?**

Three moves, in order.

First: shop it. Most small operators don't rebid their insurance annually because it's painful. Do it anyway. Get three quotes. The market is competitive and a 40% increase from your current carrier is often not the market rate. It's their rate.

Second: examine your claims history. If you've had multiple claims in the last 24 months, your increase is actuarial, not arbitrary. In that case, your short-term focus should be reducing claim frequency: safety systems, better hiring, equipment maintenance. The pricing will follow the history.

Third: update your pricing to reflect the real cost. Insurance is an overhead cost. If it went up 40%, your overhead went up. If your overhead went up and your prices didn't, your margin went down. Calculate the per-job impact of the increase and adjust your minimum margin floor accordingly.

What you cannot do is absorb a 40% insurance increase and assume it'll resolve itself. It won't. Treat it like any other overhead increase: understand the cause, address what you can, and price to recover the rest.

---

**Q20: I want to open a second location but I'm not sure the first one is stable enough. How do I know?**

Run a clarity test on the first location before you expand.

The first location is ready for a second to come off it when: (1) it runs without you for at least 30 days without a major problem, (2) the key processes are documented and being followed by someone other than you, (3) the financial systems are solid enough that you can see what's happening remotely, and (4) the operator who runs it is good enough to run it without daily input from you.

If any of those four things require you, the second location will dilute the first and you'll end up with two underperforming operations instead of one solid one.

The practical test: go on a one-week trip and don't check in. If the business runs fine without you, you're closer to ready. If it falls apart or you're getting 30 calls a day, you have a systems problem, not an expansion opportunity.

The other thing to look at: is the second location being opened because the first is generating profit you need to deploy, or because you're bored or growth-motivated? The former can work. The latter almost always damages the original business.

---

**Q21: How do I stop my salespeople from discounting to close?**

Structure and culture, not just rules.

The structure fix: change the commission plan so discounts below a margin floor either reduce commission or remove it entirely. When discounting costs the salesperson money, it stops.

The culture fix is harder. If the sales team believes they can't close without discounting, they will find ways to discount even around a commission structure. The root of that belief is usually one of two things: they don't know how to handle price objections without giving ground, or they've been rewarded for revenue without being held to margin.

Run your sales team through the price objection scripts in this library. Practice them. Role-play them. Most salespeople who discount reflexively have never been given a different tool.

Track margin by salesperson, not just revenue. Make it visible. When everyone can see that Salesperson A is closing at 42% margin and Salesperson B is closing at 31%, the conversation about discounting stops being philosophical and starts being factual.

---

**Q22: One of my crew members told me he's being paid more by a competitor. Should I match it?**

Find out if it's true first.

"What are they offering?" is a legitimate question. Get the number. If they can't or won't give you a specific number with a specific employer, the information may be a negotiating tactic rather than a real offer.

If there's a real offer: evaluate it against what you know about the market and about this employee's performance. If they're worth the number and you can pay it, have a real conversation: "I'd like to keep you. Here's what I can do." Don't volunteer the counter. Ask what it would take for them to stay. Sometimes it's not just about pay.

If you can't match it: be honest. "I can get to [$X] now, and if you're at [performance target] in 90 days, we revisit it. I can't match that number today but here's what I can do."

The thing to avoid: getting into a bidding war with your own employees on a regular basis. If you're constantly having to counter-offer to retain people, the issue is usually that your base pay is below market and you're losing people before the conversation happens. Get ahead of it with a structured pay review process instead.

**Q23: I've been running the same profit margin for three years no matter how much revenue I do. Why?**

This is called a margin trap, and it's usually structural.

The most common cause: your overhead is growing proportionally with your revenue. Every time you add revenue, you add capacity: a truck, a person, a piece of equipment, and those costs eat the additional revenue. The margin percentage stays flat because the overhead expansion matches the revenue expansion.

The second cause: pricing hasn't kept pace with cost increases. If your labor cost or materials cost went up 8% over three years and you raised prices by 3%, your margin compressed by roughly 5 points. That compression may be masked by revenue growth.

The fix requires breaking the cost-revenue proportionality. The goal is to add revenue faster than overhead. That happens when: you have enough process and people in place that new revenue doesn't require new overhead, or when you add revenue in a category that runs on existing infrastructure.

Also look at your most profitable jobs versus your least profitable jobs from the last 12 months. Almost always, the lowest-margin jobs are in a specific category: a job type, a customer type, or a market segment. If you can identify and exit those categories, your margin improves without any pricing changes.

**Q24: How often should I have all-hands meetings, and what should be in them?**

Monthly at the most, quarterly as a minimum.

All-hands meetings have a specific purpose: to give the full team information and context they can't get from their day-to-day work. They are not for announcements that could be an email. They are not for updates that could be a Monday huddle item.

What works in an all-hands: (1) the business results from the last period, honestly presented, (2) one thing you're changing and why, (3) one thing the team is doing well that you want to recognize specifically, (4) what you're focused on for the next period and what that means for each part of the team.

What doesn't work: vague motivational talks, announcements of things the team already knew, public recognition that doesn't name specific behavior (just "the team has been great"), and meetings that run more than 45 minutes.

The single most effective element in an all-hands is the honest financial update. Teams that know roughly how the business is doing make better decisions at every level. Keeping the financials entirely hidden creates a trust gap that costs you more than the transparency risk.

---

**Q25: When do I need to hire an operations manager versus a general manager?**

An operations manager owns the production and delivery side: jobs, crews, scheduling, job cost, quality control. A general manager owns everything below you: sales, operations, office, and people.

You need an operations manager when your production complexity is outrunning your ability to oversee it directly and you have someone qualified to take that on. At $2M-$4M, this often looks like a strong foreman who grows into the role rather than an outside hire.

You need a general manager when you're ready to remove yourself from the daily operations of the entire business and your revenue supports the salary. At $5M+ with healthy margins, a general manager makes sense. At $2M, you probably can't afford one, and if you could, you'd need to give them enough scope to justify the role.

The mistake is hiring a general manager before you have documented systems for them to manage. A GM without systems becomes a very expensive version of you, doing the same job you were doing at a higher cost.

Build the operations manager first. Let them codify the production side. Then when the business is ready for a GM layer, the systems are there to hand over.

**Q26: My crew keeps making the same mistakes on jobs. How do I get this to stop?**

Checklists before verbal reminders. Consequences before frustration.

If the same mistake is happening repeatedly, it means one of three things: the expectation wasn't clear enough, the system for preventing the mistake doesn't exist yet, or the consequence for making the mistake is not visible enough to change behavior.

Start with the expectation. Can you point to a specific document, checklist, or training session where the expectation was communicated? If not, you have a documentation problem.

If the expectation is documented and they're still getting it wrong, you have a training or comprehension problem. Have a foreman walk through the specific item on the checklist with the crew member, not just hand them the checklist.

If training is complete and documented and the mistake is still happening, you have a performance problem and it needs to be addressed directly with the individual.

The broader principle: systems handle mistakes at scale. Personal correction handles individual performance issues. Trying to personal-correct a systems problem costs you enormous time and rarely produces lasting change. Build the checklist. Enforce the checklist. Review the checklist at the Monday huddle until it becomes habit.

**Q27: I run a roofing company and I want to add gutters and siding as additional revenue lines. Is that a good idea?**

Only if the first business is running without you.

Expansion into adjacent services looks profitable because the customer acquisition cost is low: you already have the customer. But it also requires new training, new suppliers, new quality standards, and new production processes. If your core business isn't systematized, adding a second line adds chaos to chaos.

The test: run a full pilot on the adjacent service for 90 days. Track the margin, the callback rate, the crew hour accuracy, and customer satisfaction separately from your core business. If the pilot runs at or above your core business margins, the model works. If it doesn't, you've learned what needs to be fixed before you scale it.

The other thing to look at: does this service get sold to the same customer at the same time, or is it a separate acquisition? If it's a genuine bundled sale, where the customer gets a new roof and adds gutters, the economics are usually good. If you're building a separate sales channel for a separate service, the overhead math gets harder.

**Q28: I want to start tracking KPIs but I don't know which ones matter most. Where do I start?**

Five numbers. That's it to start.

1. Revenue collected this week vs. last week vs. same week last year
2. Gross margin % on jobs closed this week (actual, not estimated)
3. Cash on hand today
4. Accounts receivable over 30 days
5. Number of new leads this week and close rate last 30 days

Those five numbers, reviewed weekly, will surface 90% of the problems that affect a $1M-$5M business. They will also tell you when something is improving.

The mistake is building a 30-metric dashboard before you have the discipline to look at five numbers every week. Start with five. Look at them without exception every Monday. After 90 days, you'll know which two or three metrics are the most predictive for your specific business. Add detail from there.

Discipline of review is more important than completeness of data. A 5-metric dashboard you look at weekly is worth more than a 30-metric dashboard you look at quarterly.

**Q29: My best month ever was last March at $340K in revenue. But my net was only $18K. How is that possible?**

Because revenue and profit have a complicated relationship at this stage of business.

At $340K in a month, your direct job costs were probably $195K-$210K (assuming 38-40% gross margin). Gross profit was $130K-$145K. But overhead at $2M annual run rate is probably $35K-$45K per month. Net before your compensation is $85K-$110K. After your compensation and any distributions, the reported net drops significantly.

The other common explanation: timing. If that $340K in revenue included jobs where significant material and labor costs hit in March but a portion of the revenue is collected in April, your books may show the cost but not the matching revenue in the same period.

Run a job-by-job analysis of every job that contributed to that $340K. For each job: what did you collect, what did it cost, what was the actual margin. The aggregate of those job-level margins is your real gross margin for the month. If it's materially below 38%, that's where the net went.

The other thing to check: were there any one-time costs that hit in March? Equipment, a settlement, a large repair? Those will compress net in a high-revenue month without a recurring pattern.

**Q30: What's the single most important thing I should be doing at $3M that I'm probably not doing?**

Protecting your margin with the same intensity you protect your revenue.

At $3M, most operators are focused almost entirely on top-line growth. More leads, more jobs, more revenue. The margin conversation gets deferred because there's always a more pressing growth problem.

But the math is relentless: at $3M with 36% gross margin and 22% overhead, your net is 14%, which is $420K. At $3M with 42% gross margin and 22% overhead, your net is 20%, which is $600K. Same revenue. Same overhead. $180K difference in net.

Most of that gap comes from three places: job costing (not knowing your real cost before you bid), production variance (costs running higher than estimate), and pricing discipline (discounting and absorbing cost overruns instead of pricing them correctly).

The operators who build durable businesses at $3M-$5M are the ones who get obsessive about margin at the same time they're growing revenue. The operators who stay stuck are the ones who grow revenue year after year and wonder why their bank account doesn't reflect it.
